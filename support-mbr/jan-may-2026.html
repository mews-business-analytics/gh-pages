May sits at a crossroads. Quality metrics are genuinely improving: live-chat CSAT reached its year high at 87.1%, the missed chat rate held below 9% for the second consecutive month (after a 35% peak in March), and FRT SLA performance jumped to 74% — the best reading this year by a clear margin. These three metrics moving together suggests real operational improvement in how chats and first responses are being handled.

The capacity picture is less encouraging. Despite May recording the highest average throughput of the year (248 cases closed per day), demand still outpaced closures by 167 cases. The backlog (New, In Progress, Response Received, Waiting internal, Waiting 3rd party) barely moved from April: 2,425 → 2,380 (−45). Separately reported: Waiting for Customer surged from 365 to 597, and Resolved-not-Closed held at 1,818 — these sit outside the canonical backlog definition and are tracked independently. Resolution SLA dropped to its year low at 51.5%, and cases stuck in New for over 48 hours climbed back toward January levels.

Cases per property is the most visible OKR miss. At 0.67 in May against a Q2 ceiling of 0.6, both Q2 months are above target. Q1 was met (0.68 QTD vs 0.7 target), but the step-down has not been matched by a corresponding improvement in the metric.

The clearest theme across all five months: service quality and throughput are improving, but the backlog and the cases-per-property OKR are structural challenges that incremental operational improvements have not yet resolved.

The backlog (New, In Progress, Response Received, Waiting internal, Waiting 3rd party) held relatively flat from January through March — 1,652 → 1,825, a 10.5% increase over three months. It then jumped sharply in April (+600, +32.9%) before barely retreating in May (−45, reaching 2,380). The system has not yet crossed back below its April peak. At the current run rate, the first genuine month-on-month drawdown would be a milestone worth flagging.

April's spike is explained entirely by throughput. April saw 700 more cases created than closed — the worst monthly balance since January. There is no single operational failure to pin this on; it is a demand surge (7,891 created, the year high) that the current throughput rate could not absorb.

Separately from the canonical backlog, Waiting for Customer (WfC) and Resolved-not-Closed are tracked independently. The EOM WfC reading of 597 (May 31) overstates the trend: it is the single highest day of the month and a Sunday — WfC runs higher on weekends as fewer agents move cases out of pending — compared against an April 30 figure (365) that sat near its monthly low. On a like-for-like monthly-average basis, WfC rose only ~8% (Apr avg 386 → May avg 417), squarely within the band it has held all year (Jan 379, Feb 420, Mar 406), and it had already fallen back to 333 by June 2. No evidence of cases being parked to deflate the visible queue. Resolved-not-Closed has held near 1,800 throughout the year.

Median FRT (hours) — monthly

Jan–May 2026. The trustworthy number — flat at 1.6–2.9h all year. Avg is tail-dominated and understated for recent months (see flag below).

The median is the honest number, and it is roughly flat: 1.6–2.9h every month, May included. First-response behaviour did not transform in May. The eye-catching average drop (100.8h → 22.0h) is a cohort-completeness artifact, not an improvement — May's average simply hasn't filled in its tail yet. The mechanism is below; the chart above now plots the median for exactly this reason.

Why the May average is censored two ways. Cases are bucketed by case_created_date, and count_towards_first_response only flips TRUE once a case has actually been responded to. So (1) the slow responders aren't in the denominator yet — May has 2,941 cases (37%) still open vs ~1–3% for Jan–Mar, and the ones that will eventually become the 500h+ "took weeks" tail haven't responded, so they're excluded entirely; and (2) the tail is physically capped — as of 2026-06-03 no May case can have waited more than ~33 days, so the 1,400–2,976h cases that inflate Jan–Apr averages cannot exist for May yet. The average is tail-dominated, so both effects hit it hard. Max FRT collapses monotonically with recency (2,976h → 776h), which is the fingerprint of the artifact.

The same caution applies to the tier × priority averages below and to the SLA % itself: both lift partly because the not-yet-answered slow cases are missing from May's eligible set. The SLA direction may still be genuinely positive (the missed-chat and CSAT improvements support it), but the magnitude will soften as May's cohort matures. Read the SLA chart as directional, not as a confirmed +7.7pp.

Do not present the May avg-FRT drop as a win — it will reverse. As the 2,941 open May cases get answered over the coming weeks, the average will climb (Jan–Apr settled at 86–110h). This is the exact pattern the canonical FRT methodology flags: avg FRT drifts upward for weeks after month-end as late responses land; the median is the trustworthy number. Recommendation: quote the median (≈1.6h, flat), and mark the May average provisional until the cohort matures (~1–2 weeks past month-end) — or drop May from the avg bar entirely.

Resolution SLA is the most consistently underperforming metric this year — and May is the worst month yet at 51.5%. The goal of 100% has never been within reach across any of the five months; the best performance was January at 56.3%. This is not a temporary deterioration; it reflects a structural misalignment between the SLA threshold (72 hours for non-critical) and the operational reality of how long cases take to resolve.

To understand why: with an average final resolution time of 8.7 days in May, the median case is taking roughly three times the non-critical SLA of 72 hours. Even the fastest half of cases are unlikely to close within 72 hours if the average is 8.7 days. The SLA is calibrated for an operating cadence that the team is not currently delivering against. This is not a quality complaint — teams handle complex technical issues, and resolution times at this scale are not unusual for B2B SaaS support — but the 100% goal is mathematically incompatible with average resolution times above three days without a fundamental change in staffing, automation, or case deflection.

The Final Resolution Time trend is more nuanced. After four months of consistent worsening (6.7d → 8.9d, +33%), May showed the first modest improvement (8.9d → 8.7d). It is too early to call this a reversal — it may simply reflect the composition of cases closed in May (older cases from a period of lighter demand) — but it is the first directionally positive signal on this metric this year.

Looking at the Critical SLA specifically: the number of critical cases resolved within 6 hours ranged from 79 (January, very few critical cases) to 335 (April). May had 266 critical cases within SLA out of a total resolved pool of 7,871, but the critical volume itself is relatively small. The non-critical 72h SLA is the main driver of the overall 51.5% figure, and with 3,789 non-critical cases meeting it in May (out of a much larger total), the structural gap is clear.

Missed chat is the most dramatic turnaround story in this dataset. The metric went from a near-manageable 22% in February (the first full month of MIAW) to a crisis-level 35% in March, before recovering sharply to 9.1% in April and 8.0% in May. That is a 27-percentage-point improvement from March to May over just eight weeks.

January (17.1%, 550 total chats) is not comparable to later months. MIAW went live on 26 January, so this represents roughly five days of data. Volume is an order of magnitude lower than February onward, and the 17.1% rate likely reflects initial routing and agent configuration issues rather than operational steady state.

February (22.0%, 3,091 chats) was the first full month of MIAW operation. A 22% missed chat rate at launch is high but not surprising — new platform, agents learning new workflows, routing logic still being calibrated.

March (35.0%, 3,585 chats) is where something went wrong. Volume grew by 494 chats month-on-month (+16%), but the rate jumped by 13 percentage points. The most likely explanation, consistent with the investigation documented elsewhere (RV-5376), is that a routing configuration issue caused the system to fall back to case creation even when agents were nominally available. The "premature fallback" issue — where the chat capacity-sensing subflow incorrectly reported no agents available — would result in chats being counted as missed (agent not accepted) even when the issue was system behaviour rather than genuine unavailability.

April (9.1%) and May (8.0%) suggest the configuration issue was resolved, or at least significantly mitigated. Volume remained stable (~3,730–3,744), so this is a genuine rate improvement, not a volume artefact. The gap to the 5% goal is narrowing — May is 3 percentage points away, compared to 30 percentage points in March.

Cases per property is one of the clearest signals of where the team stands on the Q2 OKR — and the picture is not comfortable. The Q2 ceiling is 0.6, and both April (0.69) and May (0.67) are above it. The Q2 QTD through May averages 0.68 against a 0.6 target, a gap of 0.08. To hit the Q2 goal, June would need to come in well below 0.6 — roughly 0.44 or lower — to pull the three-month average down to 0.6. That is not realistic given May's volume and property trajectory.

The contrast with Q1 is instructive. Q1 QTD landed at 0.68 against a target of 0.7 — just within tolerance. The target tightened by 0.1 in Q2 (from 0.7 to 0.6), but the actual metric has barely moved: May's 0.67 is almost identical to Q1's average of 0.68. The improvement needed for Q2 has not materialised.

The underlying components explain why. The average property count is growing steadily (10,665 in January to 11,678 in May, +9.5%), but case volume is growing at a similar rate (7,718 to 7,867, +1.9% in absolute terms but the property base growth is also modest). The ratio is not improving fast enough to meet the step-down targets. Three documented contributors remain live: Ada containment regression (90.9% → 82.9%, generating roughly 95 extra agent cases per 1pp of containment loss), Final Resolution Time trend (longer resolution = more open cases at any point), and the category taxonomy reclassification making trend analysis noisy.

Priority & Origin Patterns

How case mix and channels are shifting — Jan–May 2026

The priority mix has undergone a structural shift that is not explained by volume alone. In January, 73% of cases were Low priority (5,652 of 7,718). By May, Low had fallen to 36% (2,854 of 7,867) while Critical tripled from 491 to 1,542 and High nearly doubled from 1,386 to 2,704. The total case volume in May is virtually identical to January (7,867 vs 7,718) — what has changed is how cases are categorised. This shift reflects a change in priority assignment logic or Ada routing behaviour rather than a genuine tripling of business-critical incidents. Higher-priority cases require faster SLA response and carry more FRT risk, so the shift has real implications for how FRT and resolution SLA are read across the year.

Phone is in structural decline: 1,267 cases in January, 843 in May — a 33% drop over five months. This is consistent with the MIAW rollout driving customers toward live chat rather than telephone. Phone-originated cases also tend to be lower volume per-agent since they are handled one-at-a-time; the shift toward chat improves agent efficiency but only if the chat infrastructure is working reliably (see March missed chat spike).

Email-HMS is a new channel that appeared in April and is growing fast. It registered 331 cases in April (its first full month) and 610 in May — an 84% month-on-month increase. This channel did not exist in Q1. Its emergence likely corresponds to the HMS product launch or a routing change. At 610 cases in May, it is now the 3rd largest email channel after Email-PMS (700) and would soon surpass it at the current growth rate. The support implications of this channel have not yet been fully absorbed.

AdaBot remains the dominant origin at 63–65% of total volume, but it has not grown proportionally with total volume. January Ada cases (4,737) are actually the year's second highest; May (4,981) is the year's highest. The Ada containment regression documented elsewhere (90.9% → 82.9%) means more conversations are becoming agent cases — this is one mechanism driving volume growth in the non-Ada channels.

"Other" Category — Deep Dive

Scale of the "Other" problem is bigger than the single-field view suggests. Counting only category_global_c = 'Other' gives 566 cases. But 1,877 of May's 7,867 cases (24%) carry an "Other" tag in at least one of the three classification fields, and 455 carry it in two or three fields at once. The multi-field "Other" cases are the clearest classifier failures, so they are the focus of this deep dive.

"Other" is not one problem — it splits three ways, and the split differs sharply by channel. Reading the raw source for all 455 double/triple cases, roughly 69% are genuinely reclassifiable into a real product category, 13% are content-less process stubs (a human-handoff or callback was logged but no topic was ever captured), and 18% should not be a support case at all (vendor auto-notifications, out-of-office replies, sales spam, third-party surveys, and Premium-support relationship admin). Each slice has a different fix — so a single "enforce a category" rule would only address part of it.

Ada is the dominant source and the highest-leverage fix. 290 of the 455 double/triple cases (65%) came through AdaBot — and 88% of them carry a clear, classifiable topic in the chat summary Ada already captured. The largest reclassified buckets are Payments & Billing (~70 cases: refunds, preauthorisations, ARN requests, Adyen/Stripe provider issues, deposits), Channel Manager & Integrations (~45: Booking.com/Expedia/SiteMinder sync and mapping), Reporting & BI (~30), Reservations & Guests (~28), Product/Rate config (~22), and Account/Login/Security (~22). In other words, the category is being dropped at or after the bot→agent handoff even though the topic is known. This is the single most automatable fix in the whole analysis.

Email "Other" is mostly not support at all. Of 67 Email double/triple cases, ~57% are vendor and integration auto-notifications (Flexkeeping/Firebase build alerts, protel and Cloudbeds maintenance, Aircall sunset reminders, HyperGuest ticket confirmations, weareplanet incidents), out-of-office auto-replies, sales/marketing spam, and third-party CSAT survey emails. These are heavily concentrated in the Flexkeeping / HMS queue, which acts as a noise magnet. The fix here is sender/spam filtering and routing — not reclassification. The genuine ~43% map cleanly to Billing, Reporting, POS, and Reservations.

Phone "Other" is mostly Premium-support relationship admin and empty call stubs. Of 88 Phone double/triple cases, ~40% are Premium-support lifecycle touches logged as cases ("Welcome to Mews Premium Support", "Premium Support Cancellation", "Thank you from Premium Team", "Early Stage Optimisation Meeting Offer"), and a further ~30% are content-less stubs ("Callback", "Voicemail. No answer", "Abandoned: call hung up", missed-meeting notes) with nothing to classify. Only ~26% are genuine support topics (e.g. printer/hardware, login, accounting discrepancies, POS). The fix is a distinct record/activity type for Premium service touches so they leave the product-issue bucket entirely, plus a topic field on call logs.

What this means for the headline number. If the ~69% reclassifiable share holds across the full 1,877 "any-Other" population, well over a thousand May cases are sitting in "Other" with a knowable home. Reclassifying them would materially redraw the category picture (Payments & Billing and Channel Manager/Integrations are the biggest under-counted areas) and shrink the "Other" backlog (122 cases at EOM) — without any change to underlying demand.

How to stop "Other" being created, and how Claude closes the gap

The three-way split tells us a single rule won't work. Reclassifiable cases need the right category captured (or suggested) at the point of work; process stubs need a topic captured at all; not-a-support-case noise needs to be kept out of the queue. The recommendations below are grouped that way, and they pair a prevention step (stop "Other" at source) with a detection step (Claude catches what slips through).

2. Standardise — a classification playbook for agents

Agents currently have no shared decision rule for picking a category, which is why "Other" becomes the path of least resistance. A short classification guide — a one-page decision tree mapping common intents to the canonical category_global_c / sub_category_global_c values, with worked examples drawn from the reclassified cases above — should be deployed alongside the enforcement change. The Ada reclassification table is effectively a ready-made training set: the ten buckets and their example summaries are the categories agents most often miss.

3. Detect — Claude-assisted suggestion + weekly manager review

Enforcement stops new "Other" at creation; it does not help the cases that still slip through or that close before a category is set. A scheduled Claude job closes that gap and puts a human in the loop:

StepWhat happens
1 · PullWeekly job selects the week's cases tagged "Other" (any field), with raw source: Ada inquiry_summary, Phone subject, Email description.
2 · SuggestClaude classifies each into a canonical category + sub-category, with a confidence score and a one-line rationale quoting the source.
3 · RouteOutput a per-manager review queue grouped by case owner: case #, owner, current vs Claude-suggested category, confidence, link. High-confidence flagged for quick bulk approve; "not a support case" flagged for close/spam.
4 · ApplyManager approves/edits in the review sheet; approved suggestions exported for bulk update. Nothing is written to Salesforce without sign-off.
5 · TrackReport acceptance rate and the trend in "% Other at creation" and "% Other at close" — the metrics that tell us whether prevention is working.
This is the weekly manager-facing artefact: a short table per manager — their team's "Other" cases this week, the suggested correct category, and a one-click way to accept. It doubles as ongoing coaching (managers see which categories their agents miss) and as the data source for the metrics above. Cadence: weekly, drafted to Lucie for review before it goes to managers.

Guardrails (non-negotiable)

  • Confidence routing. High-confidence → bulk-approve queue; medium → manager review; low → leave as "Other" (don't guess).
  • Honour the taxonomy break. category_global_c was reclassified ~March 2026 — backfill must classify into the taxonomy valid for each case's period, and any cross-period category trend must keep flagging the break.

  • Human sign-off before any bulk Salesforce write. No automated mutation of case records without an owner/manager approving the batch.
